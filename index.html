Categories

The State’s trail POIs. The parent box switches them all at once; the caret just folds the chips away. Drag to reorder · uncheck to hide. Hiding a category takes its pins off the map too, so what you can see is always what this list can show. The OpenStreetMap corridor (shops, fuel, food, beds within 5 mi) is a separate map layer — switch it on under “OSM POIs” in the map’s layers control.

Trail miles ahead

Filters the lists below to what’s ahead of you in your current direction of travel — set both to plan a day’s ride. A negative number looks behind you, which is where Prev steps. Needs your location.

Trail mile range

TM is trail mile — the fixed milepost a place sits at, measured along the route from the NYC end. Unlike the band above it, this is absolute rather than relative to you, so it works without your location.

Outlook

One hourly strip per town coming up, from the US National Weather Service (api.weather.gov). Miles a day sets how far ahead it looks and where each night’s stop falls; your riding speed (in More, currently 12 mph) sets the hour you’d roll into each town. Each strip runs into the night — swipe it left to read the overnight. Tap a town name for its full weather.gov page.

More

Long-press the map (or right-click on the web) to open a spot with its trail mile, Google Maps, Street View and a Find-nearby row. A plain tap just reads the map. This switch only controls whether that dialog can also move your location.

Average riding speed

Moving average over a day, not what the computer flashes on a downhill — 10 to 13 is honest for a loaded touring bike. It decides where you’d be at each forecast hour, so the weather layer and the Outlook both hang off it.

Your own layers

Anything you add turns up in the map’s layers control, off until you switch it on, and can be dragged into whatever order you want it drawn in. An ArcGIS URL is a single layer’s endpoint — the one ending in a number. It’s fetched over New York, up to 2,000 features, and stored as the URL so it stays current. A GPX is stored as its points, since there’s nowhere to fetch it back from.

Tap Locate on the map, tap the map itself, or pick a simulated spot to set where you are.
